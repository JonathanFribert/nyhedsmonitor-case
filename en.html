I built a news monitor for an employee at the Danish Prime Minister's Office

An employee at the Danish Prime Minister's Office was on standby during a demanding week and needed to quickly identify new public statements about relations between the United States and the Kingdom of Denmark, particularly those involving Greenland or the Faroe Islands. I built a purpose-built monitoring tool that follows news outlets and selected accounts, removes irrelevant content and duplicates, and delivers relevant articles and posts by email. This allowed the employee to focus on other work without personally following the entire news stream.

01 · The brief

The goal: a rapid overview without duplicates or hidden operational failures

The brief set three practical requirements: new statements had to be identified quickly, the inbox could not be filled with duplicates, and technical failures had to be visible. The tool was designed solely to provide an overview—never to make government decisions or recommend actions. The system would rather include slightly older content than risk missing something. If the AI model could not complete an assessment, the content was included as a precaution.

Silence must never be mistaken for a system failure.

Between 8:00 and 17:00, relevant findings are sent immediately. If there is nothing new, a status email arrives approximately once an hour to confirm that the monitor has run. As the person responsible for operations, I am automatically notified if the monitor has not run for 90 minutes and may have stopped.

02 · The solution

How an article or post moves from source to inbox

The monitor searches for Danish and international articles through Google News, including a separate Google News search restricted to the nato.int domain, and follows Truth Social along with 42 selected X accounts. Every finding is checked for age, duplication and subject matter. News articles also undergo a source check before an AI model assesses relevance, assigns a severity level and writes a short summary in Danish.

How the system fits together

View the technical details

Sources

Google News supplies Danish and international articles. A separate Google News search is restricted to the nato.int domain. Truth Social supplies posts from Donald Trump's account. On X, the monitor followed 42 selected accounts belonging to political figures, public authorities, news agencies, media outlets and journalists. The account holders' identities were checked manually. From X, the monitor retrieved only posts published since the previous run. GDELT was tested but disabled on 13 July after 15 runs without a usable response.

Filtering and AI

Social posts are identified by their unique post ID. If a post quotes or shares another post, the monitor also reads the text of the quoted or shared post and any available link previews before assessing relevance. News articles are identified by their link and a simplified version of the headline. An article from an unknown source passes only if an approved outlet has published a similar story within the previous 48 hours. If the AI model cannot assess a finding that has passed the rule-based checks, the finding is delivered anyway.

Delivery and memory

Between 08:00 and 17:00, new findings from the current run are emailed; overnight, they are queued for the first morning email. When a run identifies a high-severity finding, an urgent alert is also sent immediately. A status email without new findings is sent only when at least 50 minutes have passed since the most recent regular email. On every run, the system stores up to 200 examples from each filtering stage: recency, subject, source and AI assessment. If a stage rejects more, only the number of additional items is recorded.

Code and data

A small Python program uses requests, PyYAML, python-dotenv and Anthropic's official Python SDK. The system's memory and information about rejected content are stored in simple JSON text files; the solution requires neither a database nor a dedicated server.

Operations and credentials

During the project week, the monitor ran through GitHub Actions. cron-job.org started the program every 30 minutes. GitHub's own scheduler ran once an hour as a fallback at a staggered time, while healthchecks.io independently monitored whether runs completed. Credentials were stored securely as GitHub Actions secrets. Locally, they were read from a .env file that is not committed to Git.

Testing and cost

After the final operational changes, the solution passed all 26 automated tests using Python's built-in test framework. Estimated API and service costs for the project week were below DKK 50.

07 · Lessons from operations

Operations changed the solution

A four-day pre-deployment trial and the first few days after deployment exposed problems that a short demonstration had not revealed. The main challenge was not the AI assessment, but making the sources, schedules and data passed between runs work reliably together. The most important problems were documented, fixed and checked again.

Up to 4.6 hours between runs

GitHub's built-in scheduler skipped many of the planned overnight runs.

Solution: The monitor was configured to start every 30 minutes through an external scheduler, with GitHub's schedule retained as a fallback. Independent monitoring continued to watch for missed runs.

Two schedules could update the same data at once

The external trigger and GitHub's fallback could run so close together that two runs attempted to save the same tracking data at the same time. This caused three errors after runs in which monitoring and email delivery had otherwise succeeded.

Solution: The external trigger every 30 minutes became the primary schedule. GitHub's fallback was reduced to one hourly trigger between the external runs.

A relevant story failed the source check

A statement by NATO's Secretary General first appeared in a smaller outlet that was not on the approved-source list.

Solution: The system now uses both similar headlines from approved outlets within the previous 48 hours and a separate Google News search restricted to the nato.int domain.

GDELT did not respond reliably from the operating environment

Across 15 reviewed runs, the news database produced no usable responses: 14 were rate-limited, and one ended with a connection error. The other sources responded normally.

Solution: GDELT was disabled instead of being presented as a fallback that was not actually available. The Danish and international Google News searches continued alongside the targeted NATO search.

08 · After the project

Three lessons I will carry forward

The most important lessons were not about choosing an AI model, but about pre-deployment trials, visible failures and clear boundaries for automation.

  1. 01

    Pre-deployment trials are part of product development

    When I reviewed the content rejected by the system during the four-day trial, I found one relevant story that the source check in place at the time had rejected.

  2. 02

    Operational failures must be visible

    Status emails, source reporting and independent monitoring made it possible to distinguish silence from a system failure.

  3. 03

    AI should be used only after rule-based checks have done their job

    Rule-based checks removed most of the noise. The AI model assessed only the content that remained.

Postscript · updated 14 July 2026

Status after the first three days in operation

Since deployment on 11 July, the monitor has completed 145 runs, 141 of them without errors. All four failures were of the same kind: two runs tried to save the system's memory at the same time, and the save failed. The monitoring and the emails were never affected. The only source change is that the GDELT news database has been disabled. A manual review of the content filtered out on 12–13 July found no missed relevant stories.
